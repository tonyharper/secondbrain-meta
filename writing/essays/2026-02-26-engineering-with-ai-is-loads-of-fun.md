# Engineering with AI is Loads of Fun

I started programming when I was eight. My parents got me a VTech Precomputer 1000 and I taught myself BASIC by copying programs out of 3-2-1 Contact magazine. I don't think they knew what they were handing me. I'm not sure I did either.

I was a bored kid — read everything available, terrible at sports, not great at making friends. But programming engaged my mind in a way nothing else had. It was like flying. I could sit with that little VTech and disappear into the logic of it, make this machine do what I told it to do, watch something I built actually work. For a kid who couldn't find a place to fit in, that mattered.

My parents eventually got an IBM 486 clone and I moved to Visual Basic. Nothing grand — just toy projects that let me play with graphics and logic and see what I could make happen on screen. Pure play.

In high school I took every programming class available. Most didn't teach me much I hadn't already picked up, but I did learn Visual Basic more formally. College was Computer Engineering and Computer Science, programming in all manner of languages. My favorite class was Artificial Intelligence, and I'd hoped to go in that direction.

Then I graduated into the Great Recession.

Jobs were hard to find. I took an offer from Humana, where I'd interned, and learned how to be a data warehouse engineer. Turned out I was good at it — good enough to move into management after a few years. Eventually I was hired by Meta, and as I became more senior I coded less and less, though I still wrote a lot of SQL.

I made the right tradeoffs. I never got away from technical work — I just stopped publishing diffs to the codebase. But I missed how fun it was, that feeling from when I was eight, making a machine do something new.

The current AI moment is letting me experience that again. It feels like coming home.

## The Fundamentals Are Back

There's something else happening that I didn't expect: the data engineering fundamentals are back.

When I learned to be an engineer in the data warehouse, I was taught that good engineering consisted of four things: a data model, data pipelines, tests, and documentation. You modeled the data, you built the pipelines, you tested them, and you documented what you'd done so the next person could understand it. Rigorous, unglamorous, and it worked.

The industry moved on from a lot of that. People built enormous amounts of data without investing in modeling or documentation. Things got fast and things got messy.

Now AI is forcing a reckoning. Agents can't reason about data they don't understand. They need semantic context — metadata, descriptions, ontologies that explain what the data actually means. They need evals to measure whether they're getting it right. These aren't new ideas. They're documentation and tests, wearing different clothes.

Brady recently defined [three AI-Native Archetypes for Analytics](https://fb.workplace.com/profile.php?id=100011393418368) at Meta: AI Readiness Experts, Insights Experts, and Product Experts. The AI Readiness Expert asks a question that I care a lot most: how do we evolve our data ecosystem so agents and the entire cross-functional team can reason accurately and operate reliably within their domain? These are the people designing semantic layers, building evaluation frameworks, creating the foundational data investments that make agents actually work.

The Insights Expert uses AI as a force multiplier — breaking through traditional functional barriers to produce end-to-end analytical solutions. The Product Expert takes it further, owning product ideas from start to finish, shipping code, collapsing the linear cross-functional development cycle.

I feel a connection to all three. AI Readiness maps most directly to my data engineering roots. My work as an analytics leader has pushed me towards Insights. My work in Data Warehouse Privacy at Meta has pushed me towards Product — actually shipping changes, not just analyzing them.

But the AI Readiness archetype feels like a direct line back to what I was taught as a young data warehouse engineer. Data model, pipelines, tests, documentation — now it's semantic layers, data pipelines, evals, and semantic context. The particulars look different. The fundamentals are the same.

I feel more connected with engineering than I have in years.
