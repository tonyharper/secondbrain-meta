# Building a Sense of Safety

## The Article
Every 1x1 this week has started with some form of "did you see the article about the layoffs?" "Will they happen?"

Every text message this weekend was the same. Colleagues, friends, family - all wondering if we were going to be OK.

If you are reading this and anxious about layoff rumors, you are definitely not alone. In fact, the anxiety is rational, as these rumors are a direct threat to your sense of safety.

## Defining a Safe Part of Your Life

I've been through five total rounds of major layoffs during my career, and over the years I've thought a lot about what my values were, what my identity is, and what I could do to build a sense of safety during times of uncertainty.

Over the course of many discussions with friends and a therapist, I've come to realize something: my sense of safety is built on my community of friends and family, on my preparation for hard times, and (especially) on who I am and what I value.

My job is important - I really enjoy being a Data Engineer and deeply value the people that I work with - but it's not everything, and it's not where I place my sense of safety. This job is where I go out daily and act upon the world; it is inherently precarious. My safety is instead built on the world that I have and the person that I am when I'm not at work; it's built in the world that I retreat to.

I didn't use to think about my job like this; when I was at Humana and in the early days of Meta, my job was my entire identity. Yes, I had a wife, dogs, and family, but those were expected attributes of a rising engineering leader. Losing my job was unthinkable; what shame I would feel if that happened. But after the 2022 layoffs at Meta, I started thinking differently; that perhaps this wasn't a guarantee and I should seek safety in other things. I don't have control over what this company does, any more than you do; I had to take some other action.

Since then, I've invested in this and thought through what I value in life and what makes me feel safe. For me, safety comes specifically from my wife, my dog, and my large extended family. It comes from the emergency funds I've set aside and the scenarios I've planned out over the past few months. It comes from the fact that I am not just an engineering leader; I've been talking to computers since I was a kid, and I'm a photographer, a collector of books and records, a classical scholar, and a pretty good cook. It comes from the fact that I know that if I fall down, my family and friends will help me get back up.

If you haven't thought about where your safety actually comes from, it's worth doing. The relationships and the interests are probably already there. The financial preparedness part takes planning; if you haven't started that, now is the right time.

Having done all of this has given me a sense of peace. I sincerely hope that I and everyone I care about continues to work together for a long time to come. But should the worst happen, I feel prepared. My sense of safety is rooted in what I already have and is not dependent on things that I cannot control.

A Gap I Want to Name

Everything I've described above is built on something I take for granted: I'm a US citizen. If I lose this job, I lose income, routine, and a community I care about. But I don't lose the right to be here.

For colleagues whose immigration status is tied to their employment, the calculus is fundamentally different. A layoff isn't just a career disruption — it's a threat to your ability to stay in the country, to keep your family together, to remain in the life you've built. The advice to "find safety outside your job" rings hollow when the job is what holds everything else in place.

I don't have practical guidance to offer here. I'm not an immigration expert, and I won't pretend that my framework covers a situation this different from my own. What I can say is that I see it, and I know that the anxiety hits harder when the stakes are that much higher.
